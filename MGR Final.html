a little introduction

This entire book revolves around what a character can do, like the verbs of Braven’s system (if you will). So basically this book is about all the things that a character is capable of. It’s outlined like this:

Maneuvers

These are the super vanilla-latte basics. Every character can do these things, so every player should study these in order to know what they can do.

Character Builds (Example Personas)

This section exists to help players create a character that is balanced but still fun, even if they have no idea what is going on. For the most part the example builds are meant to inspire newer players to get an idea of what it looks like to level up in Braven. There is a lot that could happen, so these help simplify stuff in the beginning.

Gifts

This is really the meat of the book. Gifts are the special things that only some characters can do. Characters have to level up Skill in Arts in order to unlock these dope abilities. This section explains more; there’s a lot going on here.

Runes

This final section is definitely not useful or important for characters who don’t use magic. But basically, this last section is actually a reference book for all of the Runes that currently are discovered and documented in Braven. (And yes, there are even more Runes than these that haven’t been discovered yet. Nerds.) Oh yeah, and the beginning of the Runes section sorta explains what magic is all about, too (in case you’re curious or whatever).

Maneuvers

The following first section of the book outlines what a character can do; essentially the most basic verbs that every character is capable of performing. “Maneuvers” are all of the default actions available to every character. Each player should study the following section carefully so that they can learn all of the different things that their character is capable of.

Even though each maneuver is explained in detail with rules for how it is used, the Host’s Handbook actually has the tables of modifiers that can affect that maneuver’s performance.

What does this mean for the player? Well, every Maneuver actually has a bunch of different bonuses, negatives, and variables that could affect performing that action. Don’t worry: the Host is the one who bears the work of handling all the addition and subtraction. You just do stuff (and try not to get killed).

But as an example, if your character is trying to use Balance while running across a tightrope in a windstorm... there are going to be some negatives that will affect that action. (It’s going to be pretty hard.) The Host might not tell you the numbers specifically, but they will at least do the math for you (and maybe mention that your maneuver is pretty freaking absurd).

To perform an action the player must:

- Say what next, immediate action you would like to do (in narrative terms ideally). Such as, “I beg the shopkeeper, ‘I need the potion for my sick daughter. Please, I cannot pay any higher.’”

The Host host will respond and:

- (If necessary for determining your success, depending on your action) Ask the player to roll for that action, as a maneuver. Such as: “Roll a Convince maneuver against the shopkeeper. You may add your Socially Savvy Bonus.”

The player must then:

- Find Charisma on their character sheet (the Art that Convince belongs to) and roll d100. They will add their Charisma total to that roll (if they have the Socially Savvy advantage, they may add +10).

Lastly the Host will:

- Roll Willpower as the shopkeeper, add any bonuses to the maneuver the shopkeeper has (or that otherwise affect the total). The difference between the two numbers determines success.

Agility

Dodge

To Use: [PT Equal to Base Attack]

Character attempts to move out of the way of a physical attack, avoiding damage entirely. This move uses PT equal to the base PT of the attack being dodged. This is used defensively, so no roll is made (+50 added to Agility).

Jump

To Use: [2 Uses of Move]

Character expends two uses of Move in order to jump. The distance travelled vertically or horizonally is equal to one use of Move. The character is considered not moving when they land (therefore cannot use Agility with Jump).

Opportunity

To Use: [No Cost, Awards 6PT]

The user gains 6 free PT to use to attack out of turn. The character may use their appropriate Art to perform an Opportunity, or may use Agility (whichever has a higher value).

The user may only spend this PT to attack the target which triggered the Opportunity. The character must not be under the effects of stun, must be able to see their target, and may only gain one Opportunity per round. Opportunity triggers in two different situations:

Moving Target:

If a target moves from an adjacent unit to a non-adjacent unit relative to the user, the user gains an Opportunity against the target.

Prone Target:

If an adjacent target is knocked prone (face down or face up on the ground), the user may gain an Opportunity against the target.

Reflex

To Use: [Free, enhances defense]

This maneuver is optional and may only be used if the character is Running or Sprinting (2 or 3 uses of Move, respectively). Reflex is used when a character wishes to gain a bonus to their passive defense (Evasion). To use Reflex, the player must declare they are using it before their turn and it will last until the start of their next turn.

The character gains +15 total to all Evade while running and +30 to all Evade while sprinting. However, the character also loses these modifiers to all of their own attacks during this time.

Authority

Alert

To Use: [1PT]

Character attempts to notify teammates of something they have seen or heard. Using this allows allies to roll Perception again with +2d20.

Allies may only gain one extra roll per round.

Cast Spell (Whisper)

To Use: [Cost determined by spell]

Authority is used to cast a social spell by speaking, instead of using telepathic means. (Casting telepathically uses their target as a catalyst for the spell, requiring the caster to have the appropriate Lore. This is called “portaling” a spell, not whispering.)

The PT cost of casting by whispering is determined by the cost of the Spell’s glyphs (Direct, Range, etc).

Some social spells allow a social maneuver to be included in their casting (command, intimidate, etc). Note: the caster does not have to literally “whisper,” but they do need to use their voice enough to be heard by their target.

Command

To Use: [3PT]

Character attempts to influence a target into following their orders. Command is considered Coercive, which means that it may offend its target if it fails but is effective if they respect or fear the the user. This may be used defensively.

Interrogate

To Use: [3PT]

Character attempts to gather information from a target. Interrogate is highly effective when used in conjunction with offensive maneuvers (pain). Interrogate is considered Coercive, which means that it may offend its target if it fails.

Intimidate

To Use: [3PT & 2SN]

Character attempts to make their target afraid, dealing stun effects and negative modifiers.

Critical Success: 1d10 Stun, 1d10SN Damage, and -50 to all maneuvers for 2 rounds.

Success: 1d6 Stun, 1d6SN Damage, and -25 to all maneuvers for 1 round.

Partial Success: 1d3 Stun.

Intimidate is highly effective when used in conjunction with offensive maneuvers. Intimidate is often considered an attack, so it should be used with caution outside combat.

Awareness

Orientation

To Use: [No Cost]

Character attempts to gain a grasp on a situation, respond to a surprise, or wake suddenly from sleeping.

A failed Orientation subtracts from all Maneuvers and INI for one round. The subtraction is based on the margin of failure (if orientation is failed by 10 points, then all Maneuvers and INI receive -10 for one round). This maneuver is always reactive.

Perception

To Use: [1PT or Passive]

Character attempts to notice the details of their surroundings. Perception is used to spot things, but should not be confused with understanding things (this would be Analyze, and falls under Lore or Tactics). This may be used out of turn, but only once per round.

Recognize/Remember

To Use: [1PT]

Character attempts to remember or recognize anything that might be relevant about a target (in addition to noticing any reputation modifiers that their target might have). Remember must use the lowest Art Total between Awareness and the appropriate Lore Art.

Balance/Steer

Balance

To Use: [5PT + Action]

Character attempts to carefully keep themselves from falling while performing an action (most commonly while moving or standing still).

Flexibility

To Use: [5PT]

Character attempts to bend or distort their body to their advantage. The character may choose the highest Art Total, between their Balance/Steer or Dexterity for this maneuver.

Stand

To Use: [1 or 2 Uses of Move]

Character may move from a crawl to standing for 1 Move or from prone (flat on stomach or back) to standing for 2 Move. If character is Stunned, they must pass a Balance DC equal to ten times their total Stun, or fall back down. (4 Stun requires 40 Balance to stand.) This cannot be used out of turn.

Steer

To Use: [3PT to control for 1 Move]

Character attempts steer a vehicle or ride a mount, using one Move of the Vehicle or Mount. Steering determines the effective speed and precision of the movement, but should not be confused with Navigate (which is used to reach a destination while traveling and uses Nature Lore).

Charisma

Acquaint

To Use: [Non-Combat]

Character attempts to gather information from a target. This is the default maneuver when asking questions or gaining information from a target.

Cast Spell (Whisper)

To Use: [Cost determined by spell]

Charisma is used to cast a social spell by speaking, instead of using telepathic means. (Casting telepathically uses a target as a catalyst for the spell, requiring the caster to have the appropriate Lore. This is called “portaling” a spell, not whispering.)

The PT cost of casting by whispering is determined by the cost of the spell’s glyphs (Direct, Range, etc).

Some social spells allow a social maneuver to be included in their casting (Convince, Comfort, etc). Note: the caster does not have to literally “whisper,” but they do need to use their voice enough to be heard by their target.

Comfort

To Use: [3PT & 1SN]

Character attempts to soothe their target, helping them to avoid negatives the next time they use Sleep or Rest. An extremely powerful Comfort will immediately halve the last negative effect a character received that is non-physical (such as a loss of Courage or Sanity).

Convince

To Use: [3PT]

Character attempts to influence a target into doing something. Convince is considered Persuasive, which means it is extra effective if the target believes they are getting something out of the interaction (baiting the target with their self-interest). This may be used defensively.

Seduce

To Use: [1SN, Non-Combat]

Seduce is used in conjunction with a Comfort, Convince, Command, or Intimidate maneuver. If the target consents to the character’s sexual enticement and the character succeeds in their Comfort, Convince, Command, or Intimidate maneuver, the target experiences sexual pleasure or tension in addition to the result of the other maneuver (the character may choose to make their seduction pleasurable or tense). This heals the target 1SN.

Courage

Courage

To Use: [No Cost]

Character uses their Courage to resist any Authority maneuvers used against them. This is considered optional (the character may choose to be influenced, if they wish). If in combat this is used defensively, so no roll is made (+50 added to Courage).

Craftiness

Craft (Non-Alchemy)

To Use: [Non-Combat]

Character attempts to create something using available materials. Craft may use the highest Art Total between Craftiness and an applicable Secondary Art.

Explain

To Use: [3PT]

Character attempts to communicate their knowledge to another character, so that character can avoid negatives related to ignorance. In particular, Explain may be used with Craftiness when the character has knowledge about how an item is made, fixed, or works.

Repair

To Use: [Non-Combat]

Character attempts to fix something using available materials. Repair may use the highest Art Total between Craftiness and an applicable Secondary Art. This cannot be used in combat.

Dexterity

Dexterity

To Use: [3PT]

Character attempts to perform any maneuver with particular precision, generally involving the hands or feet. (This maneuver is used when no other Dexterity maneuvers directly apply to the action at hand.)

Disarm Trap

To Use: [3PT]

Character attempts to disarm a trap. To avoid confusion with other related maneuvers: noticing a trap uses Perception (Awareness) and understanding how a trap works uses Analyze (Alchemy Lore). Disarm is specifically the act of disarming the trap without making mistakes.

Flexibility

To Use: [5PT]

Character attempts to bend or distort their body to their advantage. The character may choose the highest Art Total, between their Balance/Steer or Dexterity for this maneuver.

Lockpick

To Use: [9PT]

Character attempts to pick a lock.

Pickpocket

To Use: [4PT]

Character attempts to take something from the inventory of their target. This maneuver is not to be confused with Catch or Grab, which can be used against items being held by their target.

Note that Pickpocket is merely the attempt to take something. Sneak must be used in conjunction with Pickpocket if the character does not want to get caught. The lowest value between the Character’s Sneak and Pickpocket determine if the character is noticed.

Hand to Hand

Block

To Use: [3PT or PT Cost Equal to Shield]

Character attempts to block an attack, using a shield or bare limbs. The shield used determines the PT cost of blocking and also gives a bonus (generally), making larger shields easier to block with, but more costly.

Block maneuvers force an attack to strike a certain area, (rather than wherever the attacker had intended) which is the character’s shield arm when using a shield or the limb of choice when blocking bare. Note: blocking is the only basic defense against full-unit attacks. This is used defensively, so no roll is made (+50 added to Hand to Hand).

Cast Spell (Fling)

To Use: [Cost determined by spell]

A character uses Hand to Hand to cast a spell into their own hands. They then may touch, punch, or kick their spell onto their target.

The caster first uses themselves as a catalyst (bringing the spell into reality) and then they use their hands to transfer the spell to their target. This is called “flinging.” (If the caster wishes to use their target as a catalyst and cast directly on them, they must use the appropriate Lore. This is called “portaling” a spell, which is not flinging.)

The PT cost of casting by flinging is determined by the cost of the spell’s glyphs (Direct, Magnify, etc), but the caster may choose to add a Hand to Hand attack (punch, kick, etc) in addition to this maneuver.

Because flinging in this way is limited to the range of the caster’s hands, Range glyph does not add distance to their spell. A spell may be flung by throwing (see Missile/Thrown). Also note: Social spells cannot be flung. See: Whisper (Authority or Charisma).

Catch

To Use: [3PT]

Character attempts to catch an attack (missile, weapon, or wielding arm), as a defensive maneuver. Catch is subject to all Evasion negatives (based on size, range, and speed) in the same way a ranged attack would be. Caught attacks deal no damage (like Deflect).

If a Catch fails and the attack glances, no Glance roll is made. Instead, the attack glances the hand which failed to catch.

Because this is used defensively, no roll is made (+50 added to Hand to Hand).

After the character successfully defends with Catch, they may optionally hold on (like Grab). If they choose to hold on, they may have to use Struggle against their attacker’s Hold.

If the character successfully holds on to their attacker’s weapon until it is their turn, the character may choose to Wrestle (if they grabbed the weapon itself) to disarm their opponent and take their weapon or (if grabbed the opponent’s arm) use Wrench as if they had already used Grab successfully.

Deflect (Shield Only)

To Use: [PT Cost Equal to Shield]

Character attempts to deflect an attack, using a shield. The shield used determines the PT cost of deflecting. Deflected attacks deal no damage, because the attack simply bounces off of the item used to deflect. As a defense, no roll is made (+50 added to Hand to Hand).

Grab

To Use: [3PT or 5PT]

GR/3 or GR/5 (0 Damage). Character attempts to grab an opponent and hold them in place.

One Struggle roll may be initiated each round by the defender if the Grab hits, to attempt to break away (and is their only defense against subsequent attacks after Grab). The attacker must use Hold in order to avoid letting go when their target struggles.

Note: Grab deals no damage (and does not benefit from any damage increasing Gifts or abilities). Character may grab for 3PT (one hand) or 5PT (two hands or legs), using 100% or 150% Muscle (respectively).

Hold

To Use: [3PT or 5PT]

Character attempts to resist their target’s Struggle maneuver. This maneuver may be used out of turn. Hold is only used after the character has already grabbed a target and the target is attempting to break loose or the character’s weapon has been grabbed and they do not want to let go.

If the character chooses not to use Hold while their target struggles, the target breaks free. Character may hold for 3PT (one hand) or 5PT (two hands or legs), using 100% or 150% Muscle (respectively).

Kick/Tackle

To Use: [5PT]

SM/5 (150% of Leg Muscle as Stamina Damage). Character attempts to kick or tackle an opponent, dealing damage. If the defender uses Block with a weapon, the character may take damage equal to a basic attack, to their leg.

Punch

To Use: [3PT]

SM/3 (100% of Arm Muscle as Stamina Damage). Character attempts to punch an opponent, dealing damage. If the defender uses Block with a weapon, the character may take damage equal to a basic attack, to their arm.

Wrench

To Use: [3PT or 5PT]

Character attempts to strangle/crush or dislocate/break an opponent’s head or limb they are holding on to. This maneuver requires the character to have used Grab successfully.

Crush/Strangle:

SM/3 [(300% Muscle) as Stamina]

Dislocate/Break:

SM/5 [2d6+10H + (150% Muscle) as Stamina]

Wrestle/Throw/Push

To Use: [5PT]

Character attempts to throw a target down or away. Wrestle may also be used to take something from a target. This maneuver requires the character to already have used Grab or Tackle successfully.

If used as an attack, throwing deals [Muscle x3] as Stamina Damage. Distance throwable is determined by dividing 150% of the character’s muscle by the WE of the target (however, the target may opt to use their Balance Total instead of their WE):

[ 150% Arm Muscle / ( Total WE or Balance ) ]

The above equation always rounds down.

Inscription

Read Rune

To Use: [Cost Determined by Rune]

When casting a rune, the caster must first read it. If the caster accidentally reads the rune or did not use glyphs, it will cast on themselves. The spell portal targets their chest and places randomly (as a glance would). Otherwise, the user may cast their rune by portaling, whispering, or flinging onto their target.

Cast Glyph

To Use: [Cost Determined by glyph]

Character casts a glyph so it may be stored or immediately used. The costs are determined by each different glyph. In addition, glyphs that cannot be stored will say so in their rules. Some can be stored multiple times in stacks (like Duration and Impact) but most can only be stored once.

Glyphs are visual, magical symbols that the caster uses to give instructions to runes. The caster can “interpret” the glyph into existence however they want, so long as they visually “write” it in some way or another (using a quill on paper or their arm, dancing, air guitar, or whatever). Once the caster visually writes the glyph, it may be stored or used.

Skim

To Use: [3PT]

Character reads a single written sentence of any language. The language the words are written in increases comprehension difficulty by +25 for each Rank a character has less than 3. If the character has 0 Ranks, they cannot read it at all.

Using Skim on a Lore Book (an item that contains Lore about a specific thing or category of things) gives the character a stack worth [+1d20] to their next Analyze maneuver against whatever target the book is about. Each Lore Book has a limited number of stacks it can grant and a difficulty for each stack. For example: a Lore Book with the following stats (Gar, applies to Demons and Deathless, 30 Difficulty, 6 Stacks) can be Skimmed up to 6 times, but the comprehension difficulty increases by 30 each time (or 80 if the character only has 1 Language Rank in Gar). At most, the character can receive +6d20 to an Analyze maneuver against Demons and Deathless.

The stacks will expire within 6 rounds, so they must be used quickly. A character can only have Skim stacks gained by one book at a time. Reading a different book will replace previous stacks.

Store Glyph

To Use: [No Cost if Glyph is already cast]

If a glyph is not used immediately, it is considered “stored” until the character chooses to use it. Stored glyphs appear as small, faintly glowing reddish-orange symbols. The caster may choose where on their body or immediate items the glyph may be stored. If the caster does not specify, they will store across the caster’s chest and back.

The caster does not need to look at or even be near wherever the glyphs are stored in order to use them. The storage is just a required record that the storage took place, not a means to access the glyph. The caster simply requests the glyph to be used and it will leave storage.

Study

To Use: [8 hours per Book’s Stack]

Character reads a book written in any language. The language the book is written in increases comprehension difficulty by +25 for each Rank a character has less than 3. If the character has 0 Ranks, they cannot read it at all.

Using Study on a Lore Book (an item that contains Lore about a specific thing or category of things) gives the character a permanent stacking bonus worth [+5] to all Analyze, Recognize, Forage, Navigate, and Track maneuvers against whatever target the book is about. Each Lore Book has a limited number of stacks it can grant and a difficulty for each stack. For example: a Lore Book with the following stats (Gar, applies to Demons and Deathless, 30 Difficulty, 6 Stacks) can be Studied up to 6 times, but the comprehension difficulty increases by 30 each time (or 80 if the character only has 1 Language Rank in Gar). At most, the character can receive +30 to all Analyze, Recognize, Forage, Navigate, and Track maneuvers against Demons and Deathless.

A stack does not need to be Studied all in one sitting. The character can space their reading into smaller chunks, if they prefer.

Once a stack has been Studied from a book, its bonuses cannot be gained again. The player must make sure to note these bookkeeping bonuses because they are situational and do not directly apply to Arts, but only specific maneuvers against specific things or categories of things.

Write

To Use: [9PT per sentence]

Character attempts to persuade or inform using writing. Writing may accomplish any social maneuver (see Authority and Charisma), but it requires the target to use Skim or Study.

Lore, Alchemy

Analyze

To Use: [1PT]

Analyze with Alchemy Lore is used to understand the physical or chemical properties of a target. Analyze will attempt to deduce the physical, chemical, or medicinal properties of a target. Choosing the wrong Lore Type has a penalty of +50 difficulty.

Generally, Alchemy Lore is the default Lore when the target is an inanimate object or substance. Analyzing plants or geology may optionally use Alchemy or Nature Lore. This may be used defensively.

Cast Spell (Portal)

To Use: [Cost determined by spell]

Alchemy Lore is used to cast a spell on inanimate objects, non-sentient entities, and targets that do not have a soul-essence.

Portaled spells are spells which are cast directly through the target, with the target as the catalyst for the spell. The PT cost of this is determined by the cost of the spell’s glyphs (Direct, Range, etc).

If the caster prefers, they can use themselves as the catalyst and “fling” the spell onto their target using Hand to Hand or Missile/Thrown. Social spells cannot be flung, but instead can be “whispered” using Authority or Charisma.

Craft (Alchemy Only)

To Use: [Non-Combat]

Character attempts to create something using available materials. Craft may use the highest Art Total between Alchemy and an applicable Secondary Art.

Recognize/Remember

To Use: [3PT]

Character attempts to remember or recognize anything relevant about a target. This can be used to learn a target’s Reputation or what a creature is and their Lore Type. For the latter, character succeeds if they roll higher than 50 less than the creature’s lowest Lore Check. Choosing the wrong Lore Type has a penalty of +50 difficulty.

Lore, Common

Analyze

To Use: [1PT]

Analyze with Common Lore is used to understand the psychological, sociological, cultural, political, or historical properties of a target. Analyze will attempt to understand the “human” characteristics of a target. Choosing the wrong Lore Type has a penalty of +50 difficulty.

Generally, Common Lore is the default Lore when the target is a person (non-magical, sentient, entity) or the product of human systems (such as political parties, histories, social organizations, etc).

Analyzing religion or religious elements may optionally use Common or Essence Lore. In addition, analyzing the status of a human target’s health may optionally use Common or Nature Lore. This may be used defensively.

Cast Spell (Portal)

Generally, Essence Lore is the default Lore when the target is a spell, rune, glyph, environmental essence, or magical entity.

Analyzing religion or religious elements may optionally use Common or Essence Lore. This may be used defensively.

Cast Spell (Portal)

Lore, Nature

Analyze

To Use: [1PT]

Analyze with Nature Lore is used to understand the biological, ecological, geological, astronomical, and navigational properties of a target. Choosing the wrong Lore Type has a penalty of +50 difficulty.

Generally, Nature Lore is the default Lore when the target is a beast, nature, stars, plant, or natural event (weather, disaster, etc).

Analyzing plants or geology may optionally use Alchemy or Nature Lore. In addition, analyzing the status of a human target’s health may optionally use Common or Nature Lore. This may be used defensively.

Cast Spell (Portal)

To Use: [Cost determined by spell]

Nature Lore is used to cast a spell on animate, non-magical, targets (animals, plants, weather) that are non-human.

Portaled spells are spells which are cast directly through the target, with the target as the catalyst for the spell. The PT cost of this is determined by the cost of the spell’s glyphs (Direct, Range, etc).

If the caster prefers, they can use themselves as the catalyst and “fling” the spell onto their target using Hand to Hand or Missile/Thrown. Social spells cannot be flung, but instead can be “whispered” using Authority or Charisma.

Forage/Track

To Use: [Non-Combat]

Character attempts to find a specified target, whether it is a beast, entity, or plant using their knowledge of Nature Lore.

Navigate

To Use: [Non-Combat]

Character attempts to travel to a location. One navigation maneuver may be made per day, per location (a reroll may be required if the location changes).

Recognize/Remember

To Use: [3PT]

Character attempts to remember or recognize anything relevant about a target. This can be used to learn a target’s Reputation or what a creature is and their Lore Type. For the latter, character succeeds if they roll higher than 50 less than the creature’s lowest Lore Check. Choosing the wrong Lore Type has a penalty of +50 difficulty.

Meditation/Rest

Meditate

To Use: [6PT]

Character takes a brief break, and attempts to heal their Stamina equal to [100 + double their Med/Rest total]. Meditate may only be used three times per day.

Rest

To Use: [Non-Combat]

Character attempts to rest for 30 minutes, healing Stamina fully. Rest can restore Sanity equal to the caster’s Sanity Regen if it critically succeeds. Rest may only be used once per use of Sleep.

Sleep

To Use: [Non-Combat]

Character attempts to sleep for 8 hours, healing Stamina fully, healing Sanity based on SN Regen, and healing some Health. If the character sleeps for less than 8 hours, there may be some side-effects. Long times spent without sleep can have adverse effects.

Character may interrupt their sleep (to take a watch or from waking in general) up to one time for no more than four hours and still continue sleeping as one session.

Sleep can critically succeed, which will double all regeneration for the period. If Sleep is used more than once in a 16-hour period, it is half as effective (for each attempt past the first).

Missile/Thrown

Cast Spell (Fling)

To Use: [Cost determined by spell]

Missile/Thrown is used to cast a spell by directly throwing or shooting its essence onto a target.

The caster first uses themselves as a catalyst (bringing the spell into reality) and then they fire the spell onto their target. This is called “flinging.” (If the caster wishes to use their target as a catalyst and cast directly on them, they must use the appropriate Lore. This is called “portaling” a spell, which is not flinging.)

The PT cost of casting by flinging is determined by the cost of the Spell’s glyphs (Direct, Range, etc).

If the caster’s target is within melee range, they may choose to use Hand to Hand to avoid ranged attack penalties for being adjacent to their target (see related maneuver). Also note: “Social” Spells cannot be flung. See: Whisper (Authority or Charisma).

Throw

To Use: [PT determined by weapon]

Character attacks by throwing a weapon at their target. Range (in units) for throwing is calculated by dividing the character’s Arm Muscle by 5 + the WE+PT of the weapon:

[ Arm Muscle / ( 5+WE+PT of weapon ) ]

Range modifiers on a weapon (like +5) are added after this equation for distance.

Shoot

To Use: [PT determined by weapon]

Shooting a crossbow, bow, sling, or blow dart tube is generally performed in a similar way: loading, preparing, and then firing.

First, the weapon is loaded (ammo is added), this almost always takes 3PT (exceptions for ballistae).

The second step generally applies to bows and crossbows, which is drawing the weapon (pulling the string back). Otherwise, PT spent at this stage may simply involve preparing the weapon.

The final step in shooting a weapon is simply firing it: crossbows require a trigger to be pulled (1PT), but bows simply require letting go (0PT). Each weapon determines the costs for fully operating, and generally outlines how each stage works.

Muscle, Arm

Climb

To Use: [1 Use of Move]

Climb Modifier: [ -2:+1 ]

Character expends one use of Move in order to travel by climbing on a surface. The distance travelled is two units less than the character’s Move and this use of Move costs one more PT than usual. Arm Muscle is used to successfully climb.

Exert

To Use: [3PT or 5PT]

Character attempts to lift, pull, or push something. One handed Exert uses 3PT, while two-handed or full-bodied Exert uses 5PT and 150% of Arm or Leg Muscle (character may use their higher value for full-bodied Exert).

Struggle

To Use: [3PT or 5PT]

Character attempts to defend against a Grab/Hold, Wrench, or Wrestle attack.

Struggle may only be used once against a an attack per round. The character may struggle for 3PT (one hand) or 5PT (two hands), using 100% or 150% Muscle (respectively). This is used defensively, so no roll is made (+50 added to Arm Muscle, after % calculation).

Swim

To Use: [1 Use of Move]

Swim Modifier: [ -2:+0 ]

Character expends one use of Move in order to travel by swimming. The distance travelled is two less than the character’s Move. Arm or Leg Muscle are used to successfully swim (character may use their higher value).

Wield

To Use: [Passive]

Wield is a passive maneuver that calculates if a character is strong enough to use their weapon.

The (weapon’s total WE x 6) determines the Arm Muscle required to wield the weapon.

Weapons wielded in two hands use 150% Arm Muscle. Missile Weapons also require Wield to be calculated. For every point the character has in the negatives with Wield, they receive a -10 to their Art and a -10 to damage. (Being short of requirements by 5 for a sword would give the user -50 to aim and -50 to damage.)

Encumbrance is a passive that calculates if a character is strong enough to carry their items.

The character’s (Leg Muscle x 2) is the maximum weight they can carry without negatives. If a character is over-encumbered twice over (carrying more than 4 times their Leg Muscle), they collapse prone and cannot perform any action.

If encumbered, the character’s value below zero determines the Stamina cost for every PT they spend. (If over-encumbered by -10, the character must spend 10 Stamina for every PT.) Every ten minutes spent travelling will also cause the character to spend this value as Stamina.

Move

To Use: [3PT]

Character moves 3 Units distance for 3PT. This is abbreviated as [3:3], with distance first and then PT cost second. Move may only be used three times per turn, no matter what. The relative speed of the character increases with each use of move: Walking, Running, and Sprinting.

Stealth

Hide

To Use: [6PT]

Character attempts to hide themselves at their current location. The character’s Hide maneuver lasts for one round (if in combat), or until the character is noticed by another entity.

The value of the Hide maneuver becomes a Perception DC that must be passed in order for others to notice the character. In addition, hiding is required in order to use any maneuver with Sneak. This may be used defensively.

Sneak

To Use: [Cost determined by maneuver]

Character attempts to remain hidden while performing another action. (The character must already have used Hide in order to use Sneak.) The lowest value between the character’s Hide and the character’s new maneuver becomes the Perception DC for others to notice.

Social maneuvers used (Authority, Charisma, etc) while sneaking allow any active Perception attempts to roll twice and pick the highest (it is easier for others to notice). Sneak may be used defensively, as long as the maneuver is able to be used defensively.

Tactics

Analyze

To Use: [1PT]

Analyze with Tactics is used to understand the combat-related properties and possibilities of a target. Analyze will attempt to understand what the target is capable of, in combat.

Generally, Tactics is the default Art when the character only wishes to know what sort of defense or offense a target might possess. The appropriate Lore must be used to determine anything else about a target.

Gift Abilities (Scan and Inspect) greatly increase the specfic information revealed by a Tactics Analyze. This may be used defensively.

Predict

To Use: [1PT]

Character attempts to predict what a target’s intentions are, and then deduce what they will likely do.

Predict (when used successfully between Player Characters) requires that the target Player reveal their character’s intentions only, but a Critical success against a Player Character would require them to reveal their intended actions as well.

If this is used on a non-Player Character, then both the intentions and likely actions are revealed upon success.

Predict may only be used once per target per round in combat (for combat-related predictions) or once per target per day (for general attempts to predict a target’s intentions). This may be used defensively.

Toughness

Toughness

To Use: [No Cost]

Character uses their Toughness to resist any disease, poison, or environmental effect (heat or cold, generally).

A disease may be rolled against once for every full Sleep used by the character. Environmental effects are typically rolled against once per day. When a character is afflicted by poison or venom, they have only one chance to resist it with Toughness. Medicine and magic may allow a character to roll additional times than these limitations permit. If in combat this is used defensively, so no roll is made (+50 added to Courage).

Weapons

Attack

To Use: [PT determined by weapon]

Character attempts attack a target with their weapon, dealing damage. Generally, weapons are capable of two different types of attacks to choose from (a thrust versus a swing type attack).

Accuracy (chance to hit) is calculated by Weapon Skill, but damage (added to melee or thrown attacks) is calculated by the weapon and the character’s Arm Muscle. Wielding a weapon in two hands uses 150% Arm Muscle.

Deflect

To Use: [PT determined by weapon]

Character attempts to deflect an attack, using their weapon. This is used defensively, so no roll is made (+50 added to Weapon Skill). If used against a missile attack, 25 is subtracted.

The weapon used determines the PT cost of deflecting. Deflected attacks deal no damage, because the attack simply bounces off of the item the defender used to deflect.

Dig

To Use: [PT determined by weapon]

Dig is an optional attack that some weapons are capable of which will drive a weapon deeper into an enemy before it is pulled out. In order to use Dig, the character must have already struck their foe successfully. Dig costs PT equal the weapon’s base PT and will deal this value as Health damage. Dig cannot be mitigated by armor and cannot deal trauma but does not need to roll again to hit.

Durability Check

To Use: [No Cost]

A Durability Check (called DUR Check) is a check to see if a weapon or armor will crack or break. A DUR Check is made under two different circumstances: when using a weapon to Block and sometimes while dealing Trauma.

Failing a DUR Check once causes a weapon or armor to crack. Failing a DUR Check while a weapon is cracked will cause it to break, which renders it totally useless. Armor can only crack, which reduces its MIT by 50%.

Passing a DUR Check means that a weapon’s DUR must exceed the Durability Damage dealt to it. Durability Damage is calculated as the total Stamina Damage dealt by an attack (Health Damage does not add to a DUR check).

When blocking with a weapon, the opponent’s attack is used to determine Durability Damage. When attacking and dealing trauma that causes a DUR Check, the character’s own attack determines the Durability Damage. If a Trauma DUR Check is made against an opponent with armor, both the armor and the weapon must pass the DUR Check.

Gore

To Use: [PT determined by weapon]

Gore is an optional attack that some weapons are capable of which will rend a target that has been struck as the user pulls their weapon out. Gore costs PT equal to half of the weapon’s base attack (this rounds up) and will deal this value as Bleeding Damage.

Recover

To Use: [3PT per Unbalanced Effect]

Recover is a penalty that some characters must pay if they have a certain kind of weapon and their attack fails in a specific way.

Weapons that qualify are those with at least one UB (Unbalanced) effect.

Recover will only be required if the character’s UB-type attack misses or their opponent dodges or deflects their attack successfully (Block, Catch, or other defenses will not trigger Recover). The character must spend 3PT for every UB effect on their weapon before they can defend or attack again.

Willpower

Willpower

To Use: [No Cost]

Character uses their Willpower to resist any Charisma maneuvers used against them. This is considered optional (the character may choose to be influenced, if they wish). If in combat this is used defensively, so no roll is made (+50 added to Willpower).

Guide: Example Character Builds

This section of the manual is meant to help newer players build a character. The following section is comprised entirely of suggestions for getting the most out of different play-styles. Obviously, the player is open to choose any options permitted by the game as they level up and grow stronger, but these guides can serve as a pathway that helps to simplify the dense and numerous (and sometimes overwhelming) amount of choices that are required to go into character development.

The following are the different build suggestions, as well as a very brief description of each. These builds are outlined below, divided by magically inclined and not magically inclined. Further details for the builds are specified in the pages after:

NOTE: If the character has any extra resources to spend (Gift Points, Practice Points, Shape Points, or otherwise), they are free to spend these as they see fit.

Not Magically Inclined

The Champion:

Primary Stat: Physique, Personality: IHS

The Champion is a classic leader in war: pure muscle with a personality to match. The Champion uses their bull-headedness and brute force in combat while also rallying and supporting their allies with their social skills. The Champion is valuable outside of combat as well, as a smooth talker, excellent rider on horseback, and adept driver of all kinds of vehicles. Of course, they are also pretty good at carrying and lifting things when needed.

The Charmer:

Primary Stat: Prestige, Personality: CHS

The Charmer is a socially savvy character who uses their words to solve problems, and if those fail, then their expertise with a bow. The Charmer is the type of character who might just be able to dissuade their enemies before any bloodshed takes place, or or least psychologically torment them if they can’t disarm them. The Charmer prefers distance from physical altercations whenever possible, using deadly ranged attacks in combat.

The Fighter:

Primary Stat: Prowess, Personality: CRD

The simplest and most straightforward character of all is the Fighter. The Fighter has honed their skills around any sort of philosophy, as long as it involves killing stuff. The Fighter is athletic, strong, fast, tactical, and relatively courageous and willed. The Fighter has a few things to offer out of combat: decently capable dexterity and ability to ride and steer mounts and vehicles. Other than these things, the Fighter simply does what they do best (which of course should be obvious).

The Specialist:

Primary Stat: Grace, Personality: CRS

The Specialist is the sort of character that has the capability to do just about anything without being noticed. The Specialist, while not the best at taking blows in combat, is an expert at delivering them. This character excels at precision, balance, and agility both in and out of combat; an errorless master of grace. Whether or not the Specialist is a scoundrel, they have the skills of subterfuge for good or for evil.

The Tank:

Primary Stat: Wellness, Personality: CHD

The Tank is a famous archetype: they do all they can to save their allies from bearing any pain. Perhaps one of the most important characters on any team, the Tank has the enormous responsibilty of taunting enemies into attacking them instead of their friends and then surviving until their are no foes left. The Tank is tough and indomitable, wielding a shield and most likely a suit of armor as well. The most tragic Tank character is one who dies before anyone else: this means that they lived their purpose and did so honorably.

Magically Inclined

The SpellSlinger:

Primary Stat: Wits, Personality: IRS

The Spellslinger is a practical sort of magical user, opting for throwing magic and spells onto their targets (called “flinging”) instead of summoning them into the world through their target (called “portaling”). While opponents can see the attack coming and dodge or deflect to defend themselves, the caster is not forced to find creative solutions when fighting high level enemies like the Classic might be. This build choice makes combat much simpler, with the tradeoff being the Spellslinger’s general lack of knowledge about the things of the world. Spellslingers are mosly Mage-licensed wizards, sorcerers, or Bow-conjurers (who use missile-type weapons, imbued with magic).

The Summoner:

Primary Stat: Erudition, Personality: IHD

The Summoner is a magical user who uses their knowledge of Lore as their primary strength both in combat and out. The Summoner casts spells by directly summoning magic into reality through their target. The Summoner might be a Priest, Shaman, Druid, Scholar, or Chandler or Officiant-licensed Wizard, mixing their deep wisdom of the world with an acute ability to influence others. Really, any sort of magic-user will find satisfaction as a Summoner because of their knowledge and relevance.

The Synergist:

Primary Stat: Nerve, Personality: IRD

The Synergist is the most complex type of magic user: They use melee weapons or attacks as well as magic. The Synergist is typically a Paladin, Templar, Monk, or War-Alchemist. A Synergist casts magic onto their weapons or through themselves in order to strike their opponent with both a physical attack and a spell at once, dealing devastating damage (with much more Trauma inflicted) but at a high risk: Both attacks at once are easier to defend against than two separate attacks. These characters build themselves with a steely nerve, for the long haul, intending to survive the drain of a long adventure when others would not.

What the heck are Gifts?

To answer that question simply, Gifts are special abilities that a character can learn. While Maneuvers are basic abilities that everyone is capable of (as explained in the Maneuvers section), characters will vary on which Gifts they end up with.

As a character levels up and earns more Skill in various Arts, they unlock the possibility of purchasing new Gifts using their Gift Points. This section begins with Repeatable Gifts (on facing page) and is then organized alphabetically according to each Art category (from Agility to Willpower).

An example Gift is outlined below, with a little explanation about how to read each entry in the following section:

“Rally Cry” is the name of this gift. It is an “ability” which means that it is not a passive bonus but rather something the character does (like a maneuver).

The character must already have the ability “Encourage” (which is a Courage Gift), before unlocking this Gift.

This is the explanation of what the Gift does.

Rally Cry - (Ability)

Need 10 Skill in: Authority & Courage

Requires: Encourage (Courage)

To Purchase: [1 Gift Point]

To Use: [3PT]

The character calls out to their allies and raises all of their Courage for three rounds. This costs 3PT, may be used at any time, and may only be used once per day. Each ally may add a +2d6 for all Courage checks.

Confidence - (Passive)

Need 25 Skill in: Authority

To Purchase: [2 Gift Points]

+10 to Charisma.

All additions are considered as a “Bonus” (no Gift increases the “Skill” of an Art).

Alternatively, this could be read as:

+10 [Bonus] to Charisma

Rally Cry requires the character to have 10 Skill (not Total or Bonus) in both Authority and Courage before they can “unlock” this Gift.

Each use of this ability requires the character to spend 3 Potential.

In order to gain this Gift, the character must spend 1 Gift point (and also have met the above requirements).

These gifts may be purchased any number of times (as opposed to all other gifts, which may only be purchased once each). These gifts also have no requisite skill needed in order to purchase. Due to the high cost of these gifts, players are strongly recommended to wait before purchasing.

The caster must remain in contact with whatever they are casting on for the full duration. A roll to successfully cast is made at the very end of the process.

The caster must choose which type of Permanence to enhance a spell with: Permanent Creation or Permanent Casting. Not all spells may make sense with one or either of these modes of Permanence (but the player and host are encouraged to find creative uses).

In addition to these two types below, some runes will already have rules in place for how Permanence will affect them. (The rune detail pages have thorough explanations.) The differences between Mundane and Arcane Permanence will be explained with Conjure Fire:

Permanent Creation (called “Mundane”)

A permanently conjured fire will be a real fire: it has the effects of a real fire, can be put out like a real fire, and must be treated as a regular fire would. The fire is not brought into the world with a powerful blast (like Conjure Fire normally is), but rather as a soft and wholesome flame. Some spells are quite useful with Mundane, while others are not. Conjure Bone will permanently graft bone to its subject, Counter Spell will permanently raise resistance slightly, etc. Using Mundane Permanence multiplies the SN cost of the spell by five times.

Permanent Casting (called “Arcane”)

A permanently conjured fire will remain an open portal of flame from the essence realm. The conjured spell will only be half as powerful as the typical rune that cast it, but it will recast itself if its duration ever runs out. Essentially, this spell is cast with Duration II autonomously and forever.

The danger of an Arcane Permanence is that this spell portal can be closed in any regular way: with Counter Spell, Dispel Essence, Dispel Fire, etc. Some spells are not as useful (or even make sense) with this, while others are awesome.

If this spell succeeds in casting with Arcane Permanence, five times the total SN Cost of the spell is subtracted from the caster’s Maximum Sanity. If the Permanence is ever removed, dispelled, or otherwise cancelled by magical means, the maximum sanity does not return to the caster.

Runes and Spellcasting

The following section is dedicated to runes, which are sources of magical power. Runes are written symbols which when read will cast a spell. Before a rune is read (and cast as a spell), the caster may use glyphs in order to give commands to their spell. The process for using magic involves two parts: creating a spell and then casting that spell. Each have their own characteristics.

How to Create a Spell:

A spell is comprised of two parts: glyphs and runes. These are explained briefly below:

Glyphs

Glyphs are commands that a caster can give to a spell. If the caster does not give a spell any commands, then the spell will cast onto the caster (with no special enhancements) by default. Some glyphs examples are:

Direct: This glyph allows the caster to target things other than themselves. The default range of this glyph is one unit.

Range: This glyph increases the distance that a rune can travel. If used without Direct, the rune will randomly strike a target within range.

Duration: This glyph makes a spell last longer, dealing its effects again each round or increasing its effectiveness.

Runes

Runes are the actual content of the spell. So while Direct and Range means that the caster is preparing to hit a target that is far away, the rune the caster chooses will affect what happens to their target. Two examples are given below:

Conjure Fire: This rune will deal fire damage to whatever it is cast upon.

Recall: This rune will allow the caster to see the memories of whatever it is cast upon.

Glyphs + a Rune = A Spell

So in our above examples, if the caster simply reads the rune for Conjure Fire, they themselves would immolate. But if they prepared and used the glyphs Direct and Range, then they would be able to cast the fire on a specific target (of their choosing) far away from themselves.

How to Cast a Spell:

After a spell has been created (using glyphs or not) it can be cast upon a target in several different ways:

Portaling

The default way to cast a spell is to create a portal from the essence realm that brings the spell into existence. The caster’s target becomes the catalyst for the portal, which means that they are the source of the spell itself. The spell instantly manifests onto its catalyst. This method of casting is considered default and any spell can be portaled.

Example with Conjure Fire + Direct + Range:

The caster’s target suddenly bursts into flames as the caster brings their spell into reality. The caster’s target cannot defend against this.

Flinging

An alternative way to cast a spell is for the caster to use themselves as a catalyst for a spell, portaling it into reality through themselves. Once the spell has manifested, they can punch or throw their spell onto their target. Not all spells can be flung, however. (Social spells cannot by default, in addition to some others that explicitly say so.)

Example with Conjure Fire + Direct + Range:

The caster’s hand glows with a ball of fire, which they throw through the air at their target. The caster’s target can chose to defend against this with a Dodge, Block, or Deflect.

Whispering

The last way to cast a spell is by whispering. Only social spells may be whispered. Similar to flinging, the caster uses themselves as a catalyst to portal the spell into reality. But once the spell has manifested, they whisper it (speak it) towards their target. Anyone can hear the spell being cast, but it will only affect the caster’s target.

Example with Recall + Direct + Range:

The tip of the caster’s tongue tingles with magic and then they utter strange words. Their target hears these words and can try to defend with Willpower or Courage.

A note on Counter-spell

Counter-spell works when a spell is created (it must be used before a spell is flung or whispered).

Legal Runes:

Licensure and Free-Use

This first section is a reference for all runes which are considered legal to use, but have licensing restrictions. For the most part, all of the runes in this following section require that their caster has a Wizard License, Templar Badge, or Paladin Badge. These licenses and badges are used to give the caster access to the runebooks that contain these spells.

In order to use any rune (license-required, free-use, or illegal), the caster must have access to the written rune itself. All runes are recorded in some form or another and are always cast by reading them.

In order to determine which license is needed for each spell, refer to the individual license-books (the specific runebooks).

Despite these restrictions, there are two runes which can be used freely by anyone: Charm/Enthrall and General/Detailed Appraisal. These are easily found in several different household and tradebook publications. Anyone is allowed to cast these without fear of punishment.

Split/Join Essence

Split Essence

[1hour/18SN]

Caster attempts to split a target essence. Splitting essence can work in one of two ways:

If the caster casts on another, they attempt to exorcise any foreign, sentient, essences from the target, leaving only the target’s original source essence. This effect is permanent.

If the caster casts on themselves, they won’t exorcise themselves but instead divide their own essence into an additional piece. This effect lasts for one month.

Exorcism is simple: The caster uses their Essence Lore to cast out the unwelcome possessions from their target. This does not destroy or kill an essence that is cast out, it just removes them from the target.

If the caster’s essence is split, they gain an additional “soul” duplication within themselves. This soul-essence co-exists within their body (unless moved, joined, or bound to something else).

The caster’s maximum Sanity is divided by how many duplications they have, then multiplied by how many duplications still exist. For example, if the caster has their essence duplicated and then they choose to move and bind one soul to a rock (for safe keeping), the caster still has full use of their Sanity. But if the caster’s body gets killed (which also kills all essence on their body), then the caster’s Maximum Sanity becomes halved (because half of their essence is in a rock). So because the caster’s soul still exists in a rock, they are not *fully* dead (they are only as capable as the rock is capable, so essentially worthless). Their essence could be moved from the rock back to their body, essentially providing a way to come back from “the dead” (despite not technically dying in the first place).

Splitting essence in this way becomes the cornerstone for necromancy and phylacterious spellcasting; a caster can divide their essence across many objects, some of them they can animate using spells, and then have many bodies and many lives.

If the duration of this rune fades and none of the soul vessels or pieces of split essence have been destroyed, then the split essence is rejoined into the caster’s original body. If the caster’s original body is destroyed while a piece of split essence is elsewhere, then that location becomes the new source-vessel. All split essences will return to that location at the end of the Split Essence Duration.

Mundane Permanence used with this rune will simply make the duration of the split never end (this will also keep a target from ever being possessed by the same entity). Arcane Permanence when used with this rune will either eternally cast out possessions or have no effect (if the caster targets themselves).

Join Essence

Requires: Reverse Glyph

[12PT/Variable SN]

Caster chooses a target to join their essence to for six hours. Caster retains their Erudition, Wits, Prestige, Nerve, level, Spell Resist, and Sanity but gains the Physique, Wellness, Grace, Prowess, Stamina, and Health of their target.

If the target is a living, sentient, entity, then their Spell Resist determines the cost (in SN) for this spell. If the target is a non-sentient object, then the cost is 15SN.

This rune is similar to Bind Essence except that the caster does not bind an extension of their essence (a tether), but joins their soul-essence (their source) to a target. By joining their soul-essence to something, the caster gains the ability to control that object, entity, husk, or thing (assuming it has locomotive abilities).

In order to do this, the caster’s has two options:

The caster could Split, Loose, Move, and then Join their essence to their target. If they do this, they will be able to control both their target and their previous body (because of the split).

The alternative is for the caster to simply Loose, Move and then Join. The caster will not have control of their old body (it will become a husk during this time). In order to pull this off, the caster must accomplish all of these spells at once (using Combine III).

If the new vessel is a living entity and it dies for some reason, the joined essence (and any other soul-essences) will die with it. Note that a Husk is not considered sentient or living, so if the target is a husk-body and it is destroyed, the joined soul-essence does not die.

In the situation where the joined target is a non-living object and it is destroyed, the soul-essence simply remains bound to whatever portions remain. If nothing remains, the soul-essence simply exists unbound in the location where the destroyed object last existed.

If the caster joins their essence to an entity that already has a soul-essence, then the two soul-essences must compete for control. In order for the target to realize that they are being possessed, the target must roll Awareness or Essence Lore against a DC of the caster’s Essence Lore total.

If the target is aware of their possession, then both souls may roll Command or Convince immediately and once every hour of the spell’s duration to attempt control. Whichever Command or Convince result had the highest value (against the other’s Courage or Willpower, respectively) determines the soul-essence that gains control for the following hour.

If the victim succeeds in gaining control three times in a row, then they gain control for the remaining duration of the spell. During the possession, both the original and invading soul-essences are fully sentient of the actions of the body, whether or not they are controlling it.

Templar License

The Templar is given a Pin (a palm-sized brooch) in the shape of a Pike of the Call (a wavy blade with hilt on the end of a long pike shaft) and an Enchiridion (book of runes). The Enchiridion has a chain that loops between either end of the spine and can be attached to a belt or strap for quick use. The book will also track anyone who touches it or looks upon it when it is open. The runes written inside cannot be copied by any known spell, making this book one of a select few that remain.

This license was earned because the character attended Goldwynne Academy and attained a Degree of Discipline and then studied at Goldwynne University for a Writ in Practical Divinity. Every templar is given a psychological evaluation to see if they are better suited as a Sentry or an Errant. At no point in history are Templar Enchiridions in short supply, so Templar are always given licensure upon attaining their Discipline.

As part of the licensure, templar must serve the Holy Body or the Magisterium as a Knight Errant or Knight Sentry. Errants are given Calls, which are quests that they must complete on behalf of the Holy Body or the Magisterium. Sentry are assigned to an individual expected to follow and assist that figure or they are assigned to a post and expected to help run the security and operations of that place. Sentry positions are only given to the most trustworthy and reliable templar, while templar that are harder to keep reigns on are given Errant positions. Both are considered vital to the success and livelihood of the Holy Body and the Magisterium, like two edges of one blade. Templar of one kind refer to the other kind as their Sister Blade, meaning the other side of the same tool. Both Errant and Sentry are given a superior that they report to, who keeps track of their duties or their progress on their Call.

Templar must occasionally undergo a License Review (typically in order to receive new runes or elevate their Rank). This happens at a Magisterium Office or Outpost and typically only takes several minutes. They must surrender their book and brooch to the Officiant operating the office. These items get teleported to the Magisterium headquarters for review. Generally, the review is meticulous. There is only about a 5% chance that a violation will go unnoticed. (An unauthorized person using the enchiridion is the most common and significant violation.) The player or user may spend a Plot Point per use to say that the Magisterium was having a busy day with reviews and simply missed the violation.

If a single violation is found, the Enchiridion will be returned with a warning.

If the character has accrued up to 2 violations since their last review, the Enchiridion will be returned but any requested Rank Elevation will be denied. The character must wait for 5 levels until another review can be made.

If the character has had violations on three subsequent reviews or a single review with three violations, their Enchiridion and pin will not return.

They must travel to the Magisterium Headquarters in Lamoor, file an appeal, and undergo a court hearing to argue their innocence (risking imprisonment if found guilty).

Creating Powerful Combos

Creating custom moves is often about making combinations of gifts and spells and even character’s different advantages work in your favor. While basic attacks might not be considered a combo, some actually are! Even just using a bow or crossbow is always a two-step process: load and fire. Since there are two separate actions that are required, this is considered a combo attack by default. (Contrary to swinging a melee weapon, which only requires a single action.) Both of these two actions can be enhanced by other moves and abilities separately.

Combo attacks can be daunting at first. But with these examples and guide below, hopefully new players will get the hang of things in no time.

In Braven, almost every attack ability can be combined with another in some way.

But why combine things? Well sometimes it’s just to increase damage in a single attack, but other times there are tactical reasons why one would want to combine moves.
